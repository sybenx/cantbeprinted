The History of Money

Primitive Money

Early civilizations used various forms of money, from shells to large stones. The key was always scarcity and durability. The Rai stones of Yap demonstrated that physical possession wasn’t necessary—only unviolable record-keeping mattered.

Precious Metals Era

Gold emerged as the dominant form of money due to its scarcity, durability, and difficulty to produce. This “hard money” standard led to humanity’s greatest achievements, from the Renaissance to the Industrial Revolution.

The Paper Era

The shift to paper money, initially backed by gold, promised convenience but led to increased government control and systematic devaluation through inflation, affecting savers’ purchasing power.

Understanding Inflation

Inflation is the rate at which prices for goods and services rise, reducing what your money can buy. For example, $100 in 2015 might only buy $120 worth of goods today. It’s driven by factors like increased money supply, demand, or supply chain issues.

Key Drivers

  • Money Supply: When central banks increase money creation, it can lead to higher prices.
  • Demand-Pull: High consumer demand for limited goods pushes prices up.
  • Cost-Push: Rising production costs (e.g., oil, labor) increase prices.

The Money Supply

Since the U.S. abandoned the gold standard in 1971, the dollar’s value isn’t tied to a fixed asset. Central banks can increase the money supply to fund deficits or stimulate growth. From 2020–2025, the U.S. money supply grew by 38%, contributing to inflation spikes like 9% in 2022.

Why Bitcoin?

Bitcoin, created in 2009, is a decentralized digital currency with a fixed supply of 21 million coins. Some view it as a hedge against inflation because it can’t be printed like dollars. Its price can be volatile (e.g., 20% drop in Q1 2025), but its independence from central banks appeals to those seeking financial control.
