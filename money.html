How Money Printing Drives Inflation

Central banks and governments can create money electronically with a few keystrokes. From 2020-2025, the U.S. money supply grew 38%, fueling inflation (Federal Reserve data). This process helps during crises—like the 2008 financial crisis or COVID-19—by providing funds to stabilize the economy.

But there’s a catch. When more money is created without matching goods and services, prices rise. A $100 grocery bill in 2020 costs ~$118 in 2025 due to inflation (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics). This hits lower-income families hardest, as their savings buy less over time. It can also lead to more government debt, which may trigger further printing.

Unlike fiat, Bitcoin’s supply is capped at 21 million coins. No one can create more, making it a potential hedge against inflation’s erosion of your money’s value.
